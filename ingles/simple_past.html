Simple Past

Simple Past, or simply past, is the most commonly used form of past. This verbal tense characterizes actions performed and completed in the past tense. A regular verb to stay in the simple past only needs to be added with -ed, -ied, or d.

Yesterday I worked at the mall

Last week my brother studied a lot

I loved you at the past!

Regular verbs in simple past

For most so-called regular verbs, you just need to add “-ed” as in:


Work - worked

Walk - walked

Wait - waited

With regular verbs ending in e, just add the “d”:


Arrive – arrived

Love – loved

Live – lived

Now, with verbs ending in consonant + vowel + consonant, you must double the last consonant and add the “-ed”. Look:


Plan - planned

Stop - stopped

Prefer - preferred

Two other simple but important rules as well: with verbs ending in “y” that have a consonant before, we take “y” and add “-ied”. It looks like this, then:


Study - studied

Cry - cried

And for those verbs that end with "y" but don't have a consonant before, we just add "-ed".


Play - played

Stay - stayed

Use of auxiliary did: interrogative and negative forms.

To conjugate past tenses in the interrogative and negative forms it is necessary to use the auxiliary "did". Therefore, in these cases, the verbs return to their original conjugation, that is, in the infinitive.

While in interrogative form the auxiliary did is moved to the beginning of the sentence, in negative form it is used right after the negation adverb “not”. Check out some examples below.

Examples of interrogative form in simple past

Question: Did you travel last Saturday?
Answer: Yes, I did or No, I didn’t

Examples of negative form in simple past

I didn’t go to the cinema yesterday
He didn’t come to see me last Monday
